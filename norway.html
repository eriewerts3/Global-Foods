NORWAY

About Norway

Norwegian food mirrors the history, geography, and culture of Norway. Many people often think of fish dishes when they think of Norweigian cuisine, especially since fish has been the main export for Norway up until they recently found oil off of their western coast. However, Norway's fancier dishes for restaurants, party guests, and holidays are mostly made with red meat instead of fish. This is because fish is so common, they don't think of it as desirable or anything special. While that doesn't stop them from making great healthy fish dishes for their family, their lamb, meatballs, and reindeer are really where Norwegians pull out their culinary skills. As far as sides go, they use what grows easiest for them in their cold climate. Cabbage and potatoes are very common side vegetables. Strawberries, lingonberries, cloudberries, and other berries are very tasty when grown in Norway because of the long summer days there.

Norwegian Meatballs

Medisterkaker (Norwegian Meat Balls)

Ingredients
  • ½ pound, 500g of veal
  • 7‐8 ounces, 200g lard
  • 3 tbsp of wheat flour
  • Crushed coriander seeds
  • Salt and pepper
  • 1 egg
  • 1 medium sized onion, finely chopped
  • ¾ cup, 1½ dl milk
Directions
  1. Grind meat with meat grinder, and mix in spices and salt.
  2. Mix flour and eggs, and stir until dough sticks to the side of the bowl.
  3. Mix in onion and milk and distribute it gently.
  4. Adjust consistancy with milk until you have a slightly wet mixture.
  5. Form small cakes (use a spoon to make the cakes the size of a golfball).
  6. Fry them with a good lump of butter, use medium temperature in a frying pan. Make sure the balls are cooked through, and have a light brown color.
  7. Serve the meatballs with boiled potatoes, dill cabbage and a remoulade.
Farikal

Farikal

Ingredients
  • 4½ lbs of lamb
  • 1 head of cabbage
  • 2 tsp of whole pepper
  • 2 tsp of salt
  • 2 tsp of whole wheat flour
  • ¾ of a pot of boiling water
Directions
  1. Clean and chop lamb into 2‐3 square inch chunks.
  2. Place about 5‐8 of the bigger chunks of lamb into a roughly 12pt pot with a little water at the bottom.
  3. Cut the cabbage into 8‐10 wedges.
  4. Layer 4 pieces of cabbage over the meat.
  5. Sprinkle a teaspoon of whole pepper, sea salt and whole wheat flour.
  6. Repeat steps 2, 4, and 5 to create a second layer.
  7. Add ¾ L of boiling water.
  8. With lid on, cook on low for 2‐3 hours or until cabbage is a golden brown.
  9. Add a little water if needed while it's cooking.
Kransekake

Kransekake (Norwegian Ringcake)

Ingredients
  • 1lb 2oz ground almonds
  • 1lb 2oz icing sugar, sifted
  • 4 free‐range egg whites, light whisked
  • 1 tsp almond extract
  • Vegetable oil, for greasing
  • Semolina, for dusting
  • Plain flour, for dusting
  • Icing
Directions
  1. Mix icing sugar, ground almonds, egg whites, and almond extract by hand or with a dough hook in a large electric mixer.
  2. Cover the bowl with cling film and chill in the fridge for a minimum of two hours, or preferably overnight.
  3. Preheat the oven to 390°F.
  4. Lightly brush the insides of the Kransekake molds with vegetable oil.
  5. Sprinkle a little semolina in each mold as well.
  6. Divide the chilled dough into six equal‐sized pieces.
  7. Roll out each piece to fit the appropriate mold with a finger width length.
  8. Pinch ends to seal a loop inside of the appropriate mold.
  9. Bake for 10 min, or until golden brown.
  10. Cool on cooling rack.
  11. Stack rings in order from biggest to smallest and pipe icing.
